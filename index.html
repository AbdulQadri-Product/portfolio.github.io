The Story Behind the Work

How this portfolio
came to exist.

I was working as a copywriter when I met my co-founder. He was building a sales AI at the time and his laptop had just broken down. I offered to invest in a replacement. Three days later, I asked what else he wanted to build.

He told me about Mocksy - an AI tool that could turn a sketch or a sentence into a production-ready UI mockup. I was immediately convinced. We had one other option on the table: an app connecting IJGBs to Airbnbs and apartment owners in Nigeria. It was the safer-looking idea on paper. But it would have required significantly more resources and carried more risk than two first-time founders could responsibly take on. We chose Mocksy.

That was not a structured analysis. It was a judgment call. But it was the right one - and learning to trust that kind of judgment while also building the discipline to document, track, and govern it is what this entire portfolio is about.

The Role

I came in as Business Analyst and Project Manager. My co-founder told me that was what the product needed - and he was right. My days are split between meeting notes and requirement reviews, testing features on the canvas, managing legal and admin work, building automations, and keeping documentation honest and current. The 19 documents in this portfolio are the formal output. The judgment calls, the research, and the operational infrastructure are what produced them.

The Decisions

Descoping five features after elicitation interviews revealed they were premature. Rebuilding affected components after a critical bug rather than patching them. Choosing Mocksy over an IJGB-to-Airbnb app as our first product - a resource and risk decision made under real constraints. Switching from Slack to Discord when the tool was creating friction. Classifying this as a high-risk project in the Risk Register rather than presenting a sanitised picture. These are the decisions that shaped what got built - and what did not.

The Research & Operational Work

Competitive benchmarking across six AI design tools that confirmed Chat-to-Design does not exist in the market. Pricing tier research to inform the Free vs Pro model. Legal groundwork - trademark registration via FIPO (Class 42), NDPR compliance, and CAC documentation. Building the expense tracking system on Airtable from scratch. Designing and automating a Change Request process via Airtable and Discord so scope changes are captured the moment they happen. Setting up a daily reporting system via Airtable and email so both founders stay aligned without needing a meeting for every update. None of this appears in the deliverables section. All of it made the deliverables possible.

What I Did Not Expect

How fast everything moves. How hard it is to get people to show genuine interest in something that does not exist yet. And how much of the real work is invisible until it is not done. Marketing is going to be one of the hardest parts. That is not something 19 documents can fully prepare you for - but building the discipline to document, track, and govern everything else has at least made us ready to face it clearly.
